Mehrling (2012) — Money View

Hierarchy, endogenous money, and the dollarization constraint

Monetary Hierarchy

What counts as money depends on where one stands in the hierarchy: each layer is money below, credit above.

Gold / US dollar
Ultimate international settlement asset; outside money no domestic authority can issue at will.
Currency / Central Bank money (HPM)
Domestic high-powered money: cash plus settlement balances at the central bank.
Bank deposits (M1, M2)
Bank liabilities payable at par in HPM; money for households, firms, and most payments.
Loans, bonds, securities
Promises to pay deposits or HPM in the future; most elastic in booms, least money-like in stress.

Dollarization fixes the apex constraint: Ecuador can use dollars as ultimate money, but cannot issue them.

Ecuador — Institutional Context

Seven features that shape dollar flows

01
Ecuador has a central bank which holds public and private sector deposits
Public and private liquidity structurally linked — BoP shocks affect both simultaneously
02
Private banks hold offshore external assets
Some transactions bypass official IR → Broad IR exceeds official IR
03
Oil exports state-exclusive
All oil export revenues belong to the government → public sector dominates HPM supply historically
05
Limited global financial integration
Foreign savings don't flow freely → credit growth constrained by domestic BoP dynamics
04
Public sector mandated to bank at BCE
All public foreign flows (debt, oil) must be brought onshore — BCE pools HPM for whole system
06
No developed domestic capital markets
Foreign portfolio inflows virtually non-existent → no cushion during external shocks
07

Private sector became net HPM supplier only in 2024 — historically the public sector dominated. Whether this reversal is permanent is the central open policy question.

Ecuador's Monetary System — Dual Currency & Primary Money Creation

HPM creates domestic money — not the other way around

Two Tiers of Money

HPM · Green $

Externally usable. Finite — set by BoP flows.

  • Official international reserves (BCE)
  • Bank external assets (offshore)
  • Liquidity Funds (earmarked)
  • Vault cash · Currency in circulation

DM · Yellow $

Onshore only. Can expand through domestic balance-sheet operations.

  • Private sector deposits (M2)
  • Bank reserves held at BCE
  • Created via cross-holdings
  • Created via fiscal debt spending

Risk Channel 1 — Central Bank Financing

Dollarization does not prevent monetization

"The conventional thinking assumed that dollarization imposes fiscal discipline and that monetary financing is not possible in a fully dollarized regime as the central bank does not issue its own currency."

The reality

"It is not the monetary regime itself, but its legislation that prohibits a central bank from creating new deposits in favor of the government."

In the absence of a national currency, money creation on the central bank's balance sheet to sustain fiscal spending immediately generates BoP pressures — it is the only mechanism to resolve the resulting monetary disequilibrium.

Risk Channel 3 — Fiscal-Monetary Link

Fiscal policy has monetary consequences

Funding public spending by issuing domestic debt creates money without a green-dollar counterpart. The BoP accounting identity makes this mechanical and unavoidable.

Fiscal consolidation — spending cuts or tax increases — has a contractionary monetary impact, all else constant. Fiscal and monetary dynamics are structurally inseparable under dollarization.

Step-by-Step Flow

Step 1   Govt. issues domestic bond. Banks purchase with reserves → assets: reserves ↓, securities ↑. Deposit liabilities unchanged.
↓
Step 2   At BCE: bank reserves ↓, TGA ↑. Liabilities reshuffle only — IR unaffected. Total BCE liabilities unchanged.
↓
Step 3   Govt. spends (salaries, contractors). TGA ↓, bank reserves ↑. Private sector recipients receive new deposits.
↓
Net result   M2 grows without a BoP inflow. No green dollar entered — yellow dollars multiplied. Fiscal & monetary dynamics are inseparable.

Open Questions

Sustainability, the BCE's role, and reserve adequacy

Sustainability

Who supplies HPM as public flows recede?

"The current BoP structure indicates that, at least since 2014, the public sector has supplied HPM for use by the private sector." Oil revenues and external debt have been the dominant source.

The transition challenge

"As the government transitions into fiscal surpluses, its net contribution of HPM into the system would naturally decline, potentially leading to a monetary contraction."

Shift since 2024

Private sector net positive contribution for the first time. "It is still early to tell if this change is permanent or transitory."

Liability-creating inflows expand obligations without improving net foreign asset position

Role of the BCE

BCE pools HPM system-wide

Every formal transaction with the rest of the world flows through BCE. The centralized pool of international reserves provides all institutions with system-wide access to HPM.

With BCE

All banks, regardless of size or trade exposure, access HPM for international settlements. Pooling creates an equitable baseline for the whole system.

Without BCE

"Each bank would need to manage its own stock of HPM. Large banks with greater foreign trade exposure would gain advantage over domestic-focused banks."

$6bn public sector deposits at BCE (Feb 2025) — any alternative must be prevented from expanding its own balance sheet

Reserve Adequacy

Broad IR + dynamic flows — not static stock

01

Official IR behaves differently

"The central bank cannot purposefully accumulate or sell reserves... entirely dependent on external flows." Standard ARA metrics were designed for countries that can manage reserve levels through policy.

02

Broad IR is the right metric

Benchmarks that ignore ~48% of HPM will systematically misread Ecuador's liquidity position.

03

Stable IR may signal equilibrium

"Higher inflows tend to generate higher outflows as domestic absorption rises — the system self-regulates around a BoP equilibrium." What drives changes is the key diagnostic question.

Conclusions

Five key takeaways

01

BoP and domestic monetary system are codependent.

Primary money creation through external transactions fundamentally shapes the capacity for domestic credit expansion and, by extension, economic growth.

02

Dollarization does not prevent monetization — legislation does.

Ecuador 2009–2021 proved that a fully dollarized central bank can finance fiscal deficits. The regime itself offers no automatic protection.

03

Cross-holdings and domestic-debt spending inflate M2 without HPM backing.

Distorting indicators, generating illusory liquidity, and amplifying systemic fragility.

04

Private-sector HPM generation becomes a monetary imperative.

As public flows from oil and external debt decline under fiscal consolidation, the transition must be purposefully managed.

05

Reserve adequacy must be measured with Broad IR and dynamic flows.

Official IR alone understates HPM by ~48% in Ecuador. Static adequacy metrics miss the system's self-regulating logic.
